## A Story You Probably Haven't Heard

Mr. Wang is a successful entrepreneur from Shenzhen who decided in late 2024 to apply for Hong Kong's New CIES with his family. He engaged a lawyer and immigration consultant, committing HK$30M — HK$3M into the government portfolio, and the remaining **HK$27M became his biggest headache**.

His lawyer handed him a list of "permissible financial asset" categories: equities, bonds, funds, deposits... It looked like anything would qualify. Mr. Wang thought: why not hand it to a broker he'd used in China and buy some US ETFs?

Three months later, he called me, sounding exhausted:

> "My advisor says I changed positions and need to resubmit reports. The tax people say fund switches might trigger reporting. And lately I've been wondering — if something happens to me, how do these assets get passed to my children?"

I told him: **You took the wrong first step from the very beginning.**

## These HK$27M Need a Special "Container"

The Immigration Department has ongoing monitoring requirements for CIES assets. You need to **continuously prove your assets remain within the definition of "permissible financial assets"** throughout your entire residency period.

Every buy, sell, and rebalancing move may need a compliance trail. Many people use brokerage or bank accounts, only to find each fund switch triggers a compliance question. Over seven years, the paperwork burden becomes exhausting.

**This is precisely the value of an ILI Qualifying Plan.**

## Story One: The "Compliance Immune Shell"

Imagine a safe. What goes inside can be adjusted anytime — but as long as the safe itself is "permitted," regulators only need to confirm you still have the safe. They don't open the lid to inspect every item inside.

An ILI Qualifying Plan is that safe.

Because the ILI plan itself is recognised as a compliant permissible asset under CIES, when you switch funds internally — say, from bonds to equities — **no additional reporting to the Immigration Department is required, no transaction-by-transaction certification.** The authorities see: you still hold this qualifying ILI plan.

Mr. Wang asked: *"So whatever I do inside, the regulator can't see the details?"*

They don't need to. The plan is already the compliant container. What happens inside is a professional matter between you and your fund manager.

Brokerage accounts can't offer this. Every securities trade must be recorded and verified. **Over seven years, without this immune shell, compliance costs can be staggeringly high.**

## Story Two: The Truth About Fees

Many people frown hearing ILI's 2.35% annual fee rate. "Isn't that quite high?"

My response: *"How much do you actually pay at your broker each year?"*

**They usually go quiet.** Because they've never calculated it.

**Hidden fees at banks and brokerages:**
- Subscription Fee: 0.5%–5% on purchases
- Bid-Offer Spread: 1%–2% quietly eaten on entry
- Ongoing Fund Charge: 1%–2%/year, deducted from NAV invisibly
- Platform Custody Fee: 0.3%–0.5%/year
- Switching Fee: 0.5%–1% per switch
- Advisory Fee: 0.5%–1%/year, sometimes with performance fees

Total real cost: often **2.5%–4%/year**, spread across charges and spreads so **you never see a clear bill.**

**ILI's transparent structure:**
- Internal fund switches: **zero cost**, unlimited times
- All-in annual rate: approximately **2.35%** for years 1–5
- **6% bonus units on day one**: HK$27M × 6% = **HK$1.62 million** added to your account immediately, before any market return
- **From Year 6 onwards**: loyalty bonuses — additional reward units added annually, the longer you hold, the more you receive, further reducing your effective fee rate

**ILI is not "expensive" — it is transparent, with clear rewards and zero switching costs.** Compared to banks and brokers whose billing you can never fully decipher, ILI is the more honest partner.

## Story Three: What Happens to That Money After You're Gone?

Mr. Chan, a Malaysian-Chinese businessman, obtained CIES residency. The sole decision-maker in a family business, with two children in university, the HK$27M ILI account was managed entirely by him.

In early 2025, he passed away suddenly during a business trip.

**If the HK$27M had been in a bank or securities account:**
Accounts frozen. Probate in Malaysia and Hong Kong — death certificates, family relationship proof, tax clearance... typically **6 to 18 months**, assets completely inaccessible. Inheritance tax may apply depending on jurisdiction.

**Because the HK$27M was in an ILI plan:**
Mr. Chan had designated a **Second Life Assured** (spouse) and **Beneficiaries** (two children) when setting up the policy. Upon death, the insurer paid the death benefit directly to the beneficiaries — no probate, **typically completed within weeks of submitting full documentation.**

This is ILI's built-in "mini-trust" function. Not a formal trust — but in succession efficiency, it often outperforms a traditional will-based trust.

**Within an ILI plan, you can:**
- Designate a Second Life Assured (typically spouse) — policy continues in their name, assets don't trigger succession
- Update Beneficiaries at any time
- The policy is independently protected under Hong Kong insurance law, ring-fenced from other estate assets

**ILI is a complete tool — flexible investment while alive, seamless succession after death.**
